The **Vienna Convention on the Law of Treaties (VCLT)** is an international agreement that regulates treaties among sovereign states. It is often called the *“treaty on treaties”* because it establishes comprehensive rules for drafting, interpreting, and enforcing treaties.

## Background

- Adopted on **May 23, 1969** in Vienna, Austria.  
- Entered into force on **January 27, 1980**.  
- Depositary: **UN Secretary‑General**.  
- Drafted by the International Law Commission of the United Nations.  
- Languages: Arabic, Chinese, English, French, Russian.  

## Membership

- **Signatories**: 45 states (initially).  
- **Parties**: 118 states as of September 2025.  
- Some major powers, including the **United States**, signed but never ratified.  

## Key Principles

1. **Definition of a Treaty** – An international agreement in writing between states governed by international law.  
2. **Pacta Sunt Servanda** – Treaties must be observed in good faith.  
3. **Consent** – States are bound only if they consent to a treaty.  
4. **Invalidity of Treaties** – Treaties are void if obtained by coercion, conflict with jus cogens (peremptory norms), or fraud.  
5. **Interpretation Rules** – Treaties must be interpreted in good faith according to their ordinary meaning, context, and purpose.  
6. **Termination and Suspension** – Conditions under which treaties may end or be suspended.  

## Importance

- Provides a **legal framework for international relations**.  
- Ensures consistency and predictability in treaty law.  
- Serves as the foundation for resolving disputes over treaties.  
- Influences both customary international law and the practice of states.  

## Legacy

* The VCLT remains the cornerstone of modern treaty law.  
* It has shaped international diplomacy, trade agreements, and peace settlements.  
* Even non‑parties often follow its principles as customary international law.  

---

*The Vienna Convention on the Law of Treaties is a fundamental document in international law, ensuring that agreements between nations are respected, interpreted fairly, and upheld in good faith.*
