## Definition
**Mohandas Karamchand Gandhi**, widely known as **Mahatma Gandhi** (“Great Soul”), was an Indian lawyer, nationalist, and political ethicist. He became the leader of India’s nonviolent independence movement against British colonial rule and is regarded as one of the most influential figures of the 20th century.

## Early Life
- Born: **October 2, 1869**, in Porbandar, Gujarat, India.  
- Studied law in London, called to the bar in 1891.  
- Worked in South Africa (1893–1914), where he developed his philosophy of **satyagraha** (truth-force or nonviolent resistance).  

## Role in India’s Independence
- Returned to India in **1915** and soon became a leader in the Indian National Congress.  
- Advocated for **nonviolent civil disobedience** against British rule.  
- Key movements:  
  - **Non-Cooperation Movement (1920–1922)**  
  - **Salt March (1930)** – protest against British salt monopoly.  
  - **Quit India Movement (1942)** – demand for immediate independence.  

## Philosophy
- **Satyagraha** – resistance through nonviolence and truth.  
- **Ahimsa** – principle of non-harm and compassion.  
- Emphasized **self-reliance**, simple living, and rural development.  

## Legacy
- Instrumental in India achieving independence in **1947**.  
- Inspired global leaders like Martin Luther King Jr. and Nelson Mandela.  
- Assassinated on **January 30, 1948**, in New Delhi by Nathuram Godse.  
- Remembered as the “Father of the Nation” in India.  

---

*Mahatma Gandhi’s life and teachings continue to inspire movements for peace, justice, and freedom worldwide.*
